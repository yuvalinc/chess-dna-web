What a Chess Analysis App Needs to Do in 2026

Engine evaluation has been a solved problem for a decade. Any modern chess app can hand you a Stockfish number for any position. The bar in 2026 is higher: a real analysis app has to turn raw engine output into a list of specific, ranked, drillable weaknesses that come from your games. Four jobs:

  1. Aggregate across many games. A blunder on Tuesday is noise. The same blunder showing up six times across fifty games is a diagnosis. Single-game review tools can't see this.
  2. Classify mistakes into named patterns. "Blunder" is a category of one. "Missed knight fork on f7" is actionable. You need a taxonomy you can count.
  3. Rank by rating lost. You don't have time to fix every leak — you have time to fix the biggest one. The app has to do the bookkeeping.
  4. Queue real replay positions. Knowing the weakness is half the value. The other half is drilling the exact shapes from your own games where the weakness fired.

Chess.com Game Review

Chess.com Game Review is the best per-game review surface on the web. It walks you through a single game with engine evaluation, "key moments," and natural-language commentary on individual moves. The Coach feature explains that one game better than any free alternative. The ceiling is that it operates at the game level: you can review games one at a time, but you don't get a ranked list of recurring weaknesses across the last fifty. Game Review also gates most of the deeper coaching behind the Diamond subscription, with one free review per day for non-paying members.

Aimchess

Aimchess sits one level up: it ingests your Chess.com or Lichess history and produces aggregate metrics — blunder rate, time-management quality, opening accuracy, "calculation" score, and so on. The dashboards are clean and the weekly challenges are nicely packaged. What it doesn't do is extract named tactical patterns from your mistakes and queue the exact positions where they fired. The drills it sends you to are generic puzzle sets selected to match your overall skill scores, not replay positions taken from your own games. Like Chess.com's Game Review, full access is paid.

Chess DNA's pipeline

Chess DNA is built around four stages that turn raw game history into a ranked, drillable improvement plan. Each stage is automatic — the only input is your Chess.com or Lichess username on first connect.

  1. Import. Your full game history loads on first connect, and new games keep importing as you play. PGN file imports are supported for games from other clients or over-the-board sessions.
  2. Engine analysis. Every imported game is run through Stockfish 17 in your browser via a Web Worker, at depth 18, with per-move evaluations stored. No "one game per day" cap, no waiting in a queue.
  3. Pattern classification. Every mistake is classified into one of 13 named patterns: Missed Tactic, Missed Pin, Missed Fork, Missed Skewer, Missed Mate, Trapped Piece, Discovered Attack, Hanging Pieces, Back Rank Weakness, King Safety, Endgame Technique, Opening Inaccuracy, Time Pressure Blunder. Patterns are ranked by rating cost.
  4. Replay. For the top pattern, Chess DNA queues replay positions taken directly from your own games — the exact spots where the pattern fired. You drill until recognition is automatic, then the next-most-expensive pattern surfaces.

On top of those four stages, the 8-dimension Skill Radar (Openings, Tactics, Defense, Positional, Endgame, Calculation, Time Management, Resilience) scores you 0–99 in each dimension and recomputes as new games come in. Rank tiers — Pawn, Knight, Bishop, Rook, Queen, King — give you a visible league you're climbing as your weakest dimension improves. Which Chess Analysis App Should You Use?

The honest answer is that these tools mostly stack rather than compete. Chess.com is where you play and where you do per-game review with their Coach. Aimchess is fine if you want aggregate metrics and weekly challenge packs. Chess DNA is the aggregation layer that does the job neither of them does — it reads your whole history, names the patterns, ranks them by rating lost, and queues the exact positions from your games where the patterns fired. If you only have time for one analysis app, the one that automates the bookkeeping you would never do by hand is the one worth using.
