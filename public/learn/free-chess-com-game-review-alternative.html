How to Import Your Chess.com Games into Chess DNA

You can be looking at your first Chess DNA analysis within a couple of minutes. There's no PGN export, no file shuffling, no account linking beyond entering your Chess.com username.

  1. Open Chess DNA and start the import flow. Go to chessdna.app and sign up with email or Google. The onboarding screen asks you to connect your games — choose Import from Chess.com. [Screenshot: Chess DNA onboarding screen with "Import from Chess.com" button]
  2. Enter your Chess.com username. Type the exact username you play under on chess.com (it's case-insensitive). Chess DNA uses the public Chess.com API to fetch your archives — you don't need to share a password and you don't need to connect via OAuth. [Screenshot: Username input field with example "magnuscarlsen"]
  3. Pick how much history to import. By default Chess DNA imports your last several months. You can extend the window to your full history if you have a large archive — pattern detection becomes more reliable the more games you import, so we recommend starting with at least 50 games. [Screenshot: History window selector showing "Last 3 months / Last 6 months / All games" options]
  4. Let Stockfish analyze every game. Analysis runs in your browser via a Web Worker, so games are processed in the background while you keep using the app. You'll see a progress indicator as each game is scored at depth 18 and each mistake is classified into a pattern. Nothing leaves your device for the engine step. [Screenshot: Analysis progress bar with "47 of 120 games analyzed"]
  5. Open your Patterns view. Once enough games are analyzed, the Patterns page surfaces your ranked weakness list — which named patterns are firing most often and how much rating each one is estimated to have cost you. Tap any pattern to enter the replay queue: positions taken directly from your own games where the pattern fired, ready to drill. [Screenshot: Patterns page with ranked list — "Missed Fork", "Back Rank Weakness", "Hanging Pieces"]

The whole import-to-first-insight loop typically takes 5–15 minutes depending on how many games you bring. New games you play on Chess.com after the initial import are pulled automatically — you don't have to re-import.

Why Bother With a Second Tool?

Chess.com Game Review is good at what it does: a single-game narrative walkthrough with engine evaluation and coach commentary. That's useful, and on the free plan you get one of those a day. The gap is that improvement happens at the aggregate level — you can review one game beautifully and still not know that you've missed the same kind of knight fork eight times in the last three weeks. The point of a corpus-level tool is to do that counting for you. Chess DNA does it for free, on every game you import, and tells you which leak to seal first.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Chess DNA really free?

Yes. Chess DNA is free to use — there is no daily cap, no per-game review limit, and no paywall on engine analysis or pattern detection. Connect a Chess.com or Lichess username, import your games, and the full analysis loop is available at no cost. Shared API keys for the AI features are included, so you don't need to bring your own.

Can I import my Chess.com games?

Yes. Chess DNA imports your full Chess.com history directly from your username — you don't need to export PGNs by hand. Enter your Chess.com username once on the import screen and Chess DNA pulls your game history into the app, then continues importing new games as you play them. PGN file uploads are also supported if you have games from other sources.

How does Chess DNA differ from chess.com game review?

Chess.com Game Review walks you through one game at a time with engine evaluation and coach commentary, with the free plan capped at 1 review per day and the rest gated behind Diamond ($10/mo). Chess DNA runs Stockfish 17 on every game in your imported history with no cap, classifies every mistake into one of 13 named patterns (Missed Fork, Back Rank Weakness, Hanging Pieces, Time Pressure Blunder, etc.), and ranks those patterns by how much rating each one has cost you across your whole corpus. It answers a different question — not "what happened in this game" but "what's wrong across all of them."

Does Chess DNA show me my recurring mistakes?

Yes — recurring mistakes are the core of the product. Chess DNA classifies every mistake from your imported games into 13 named patterns, counts how many times each one fires across your history, and ranks them by the estimated rating loss they've cost you. The most expensive recurring pattern surfaces at the top of your improvement plan, with a replay queue of the exact positions from your own games where it happened, so you can drill the shape that keeps hurting you.
